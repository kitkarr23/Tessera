The approach before the message
The communications strategy defines how this engagement communicates — not what it says. It guides every message, every channel choice, and every sequencing decision throughout the programme. The sponsor approves this before a single message is written.

Module 2  ·  Message architecture
The story this engagement tells
Message architecture is not individual messages — it is the structure everything follows. The case for change in plain language. The WIIFM for each group. The honest unknown. The narrative arc from announcement through to adoption. Everything else is built from this.

WIIFM — what is in it for me — by stakeholder group
The case for change is the organisational story. WIIFM is the individual story — what does this specifically mean for this person in their role, in their location, in their cultural context. One group may experience this as opportunity. Another experiences it as loss. Both are true and both need to be named.
Narrative arc — how the story evolves
The narrative shifts as the engagement progresses. What we say in the announcement phase is different from what we say in the training phase, which is different from what we say post go-live. This arc keeps the story coherent across the whole timeline.
Module 3  ·  Cascade model
How the message travels
Every person in this organisation is a messenger for someone below them. The cascade model maps how messages move from leadership intent to frontline reality — who carries which message, in what form, with what support, and at what point. Managers are not a channel. They are a communication system that requires investment.
Message cascade — who tells whom and how
Manager enablement — what each level needs to cascade effectively
Managers cannot cascade what they do not understand or have not processed themselves. Each level needs its own pack — not the same content as the communication above it, but a version designed for someone who has to answer questions about it.
Change network communications
If a change champion or change network exists or is planned, they require their own communication stream — briefed ahead of the general population, equipped to answer questions from peers, and connected back to the practitioner for feedback.
External and project stakeholder communications
Communications that go beyond the internal population — board members, external partners, regulators, customers, or the project team itself. Flagged here so they are not designed in isolation from the internal communications strategy.
Listening and feedback mechanisms
Communications is a two-way system. How will this organisation hear back from people after messages go out? What channels exist for questions? How will feedback reach the practitioner and the sponsor?
Draft messages — for leadership to react to
First drafts calibrated to each audience. Not final communications — starting points that surface where the real disagreements are before anything goes out. The practitioner edits. The sponsor marks up. Approval state tracks where each draft sits.
