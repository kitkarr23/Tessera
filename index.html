The name
Why Tessera?
Tessera
Latin  ·  noun  ·  from Greek tessares (four-sided)
A tessera is a single tile in a mosaic — a small, distinct piece that is complete in itself and essential to the whole picture. Remove one tessera and the image is incomplete. Place each one with care and the result is something beautiful, coherent, and greater than the sum of its parts.

The name comes from the firm that built this tool: Mosaic Bridge Advisory. The mosaic metaphor runs through everything we do — the belief that every person in an organisation is a distinct piece, with their own identity, context, and contribution. No two tiles are the same. The job of change management is to understand each one and design an experience that honours that.

The bridge piece matters too. Tessera is built to bridge the gap between strategic intent at the top and lived human experience at every level below it. That gap is where most change fails.

What this is
A consulting delivery engine — not another CM tool.

Most change management tools are built around the project — the timeline, the milestones, the deliverables. People are a variable in that model. A stakeholder group to be managed.

Tessera flips that entirely. The project is the context. The person is the center. Every output the system generates — every communication, every training experience, every leadership message, every readiness check — starts with a specific human being and works outward from there.

"The accountant in São Paulo who has been doing the same reconciliation process for 11 years and is about to have it replaced by a system she didn't choose deserves a different experience than the IT manager in Singapore who already knows the technical side cold but hasn't been told how his role is changing."

Tessera knows the difference. And it designs for it.

The system is practitioner-led — the consultant is in the room, building relationships, reading what no form can capture. Tessera handles the analytical, generative, and production work so the practitioner has the time and headspace to be fully human in the moments that require it.

The differentiators
What makes this different from everything else out there.
Person-centered, not project-centered
Every output is generated for a specific person — their role, their context, their WIIFM, their cultural signals, their learning style. Generic outputs are structurally impossible because the system always knows who it is designing for.
Cultural nuance as a design requirement
Not a nationality checkbox. A set of observed signals about how this person relates to authority, disagreement, group dynamics, and change — at the individual, team, department, and regional level. Culture is treated as dynamic, not fixed.
The practitioner's human read is captured
The things that don't show up in notes — the emotional texture of a conversation, the moment something landed differently, the magic tick that made the change suddenly real and relevant for this person. These are captured in a structured debrief layer and fed into every output.
Designed for global, AI-accelerated reality
Built for organisations where change is constant and simultaneous — where the same ERP rollout is hitting a team in Dublin, a field crew in Houston, and a finance group in São Paulo at the same time, under the same deadline, with completely different needs.
The methodology is the IP
The questions being asked, the way cultural context gets woven into design, the logic that connects a person profile to an experience recommendation — that is the practitioner's intellectual property, encoded into the system. The AI does the production. The methodology belongs to the consultant.
Non-linear by design
Recognises that practitioners enter engagements at different points — discovery, planning, execution, or recovery. The system meets you where you are and flags what's missing without blocking progress. Reality is messy. The tool accounts for that.
The foundation
Grounded in credible sources — not built on opinion.

Every diagnostic question, cultural signal, and design recommendation in Tessera is traceable to established research. The sources below are not window dressing — they are the intellectual architecture of the system. When a client asks "where does this come from," the answer is specific, defensible, and deep.

Prosci / Jeff Hiatt
ADKAR Model
The readiness framework underlying every person profile — Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement. Also the source for sponsorship as the primary predictor of adoption outcomes.
Change management
William Bridges
Managing Transitions / The Transition Model
The distinction between external change and internal transition — and why every change involves an ending that must be acknowledged before a new beginning can take hold. Foundational to the gains and losses field in the person profile.
Change management
John Kotter
Leading Change / 8-Step Model
Urgency, coalition-building, and sustaining momentum. Informs the pressure and sponsorship sections of the project context module.
Change management
Geert Hofstede
Culture's Consequences / The 6 Dimensions
Power distance, individualism, uncertainty avoidance — the dimensions that shape how people in different cultural contexts relate to authority, change, and dissent. Used as hypothesis generators, never as labels.
Culture
Erin Meyer
The Culture Map
Eight practical scales — communicating, evaluating, leading, deciding, trusting, disagreeing, scheduling — that translate directly into how people experience change in organisations. The most actionable cultural framework in the system.
Culture
Shalom Schwartz
Basic Human Values / Cultural vs Individual Values
The research that supports the central design principle: you cannot read an individual from their national or ethnic background. Individual-level values are distinct from cultural-level patterns — and Tessera is built to capture both.
Culture
Malcolm Knowles
Andragogy — Adult Learning Theory
Adults learn through connection to what they already know and care about. The foundation for learning modality design and the "magic tick" concept — finding the frame that makes abstract change personally relevant.
Adult learning
David Kolb
Experiential Learning Cycle
Learning as a cycle of experience, reflection, conceptualisation, and experimentation. Shapes how training journeys are sequenced in Tessera — connecting to lived experience before introducing new frameworks.
Adult learning
Amy Edmondson
The Fearless Organisation / Psychological Safety
Why people in different power positions within the same organisation will experience the same change differently — and why team health is a variable in change outcomes, not a background condition.
Human dynamics
Brené Brown
Dare to Lead / The BRAVING Inventory
Vulnerability, trust, and belonging as organisational forces. People engage with change when they feel seen as whole humans — the research behind the human read layer and the practitioner's debrief questions.
Human dynamics
Adam Grant
Think Again / Originals
Psychological flexibility, rethinking, and the conditions under which people genuinely reconsider their views. Applied to how Tessera approaches resistance — not as a problem to overcome, but as a signal about what someone needs to unlearn before they can move forward.
Human dynamics
Lily Zheng
DEI Deconstructed / Reconstructing DEI
Outcomes over intentions — the principle that good intentions with unchecked assumptions consistently produce poor outcomes for the people most affected. The foundation for the assumptions field in project context and the equity lens throughout the system.
Equity & inclusion
The build
Where we are and where we are going.

Tessera is being built module by module. The diagnostic foundation comes first — because the quality of what the system generates downstream is only as good as the quality of what it captures upfront.

1
Project context intake
The landscape before the listening begins. Nature of change, strategic intent, pressure and conditions, culture signals, change saturation, entry point, and assumptions. Generates early anchors — things that are already determined before a single interview happens.
Built
2
Person profile
One profile per person in the listening tour. Two layers — structured capture for the note-taker (identity, impact, readiness, style) and the practitioner's human read after the conversation (emotional texture, change fingerprint, what makes them tick, the magic tick).
Built
3
Stakeholder mapping
Groups the person profiles into stakeholder segments. Roles today vs tomorrow, collective impact profile, group-level cultural signals, and the change experience each group needs.
Up next
4
Experience design generator
The first AI generation layer. Takes modules 1–3 and produces tailored communications approach, engagement strategy, training modality recommendations, adoption plan, and sustainability design — per group and per person.
Coming
5
Diagnostic output and story
The synthesised deliverable. The practitioner's narrative, the readiness picture, the recommendations, the roadmap. The thing you walk into a client room with.
Coming
+
Agentic layer — five specialist agents
Communications architect. Change readiness and adoption agent. Learning architect (including role-specific training deliverables). Leadership engagement agent. Orchestration layer that holds the state of the engagement and surfaces what needs attention. This is where Tessera becomes a full consulting delivery system.

Try it
Two modules ready to test.

Module 1
Project context
Five steps: landscape, pressure and conditions, culture signals, entry point and gaps, and a project snapshot with early anchors. Takes 10–15 minutes with a real project in mind.
Module 2
Person profile
Six steps: identity, impact, readiness, style, the human read, and the complete profile. Run it for one person. The magic tick question on step 5 is the one to sit with. Takes 10–15 minutes per person.
